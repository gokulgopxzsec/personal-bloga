shruthi called me at 11pm again. she always calls late. i think that's when she finally sits down after packing orders and feeding her cat and all the things.

she said "i lost three orders this week i swear i wrote them down but i cant find the notebook"

okay let me build something. a page. one link. just put your earrings there and people can click and pay. no more notebook.

that was four months ago.

13 sellers now. two months since the first one went live. not 13 in four months. 13 in two months. the first two months was just me building stuff and showing it to shruthi and her saying "no this is confusing" and me going back and fixing it.

at ₹99 a month that's ₹1,287 of monthly revenue. i want to type a bigger number here. i don't have one. that's the whole point of this blog.

two of the 13 sellers i have never met. they just found it. someone sent them a link and they clicked and signed up.

that specific thing. someone sending a link. hold that thought, because the rest of this post is me learning it the expensive way.

## i spent money on ads

₹1,978.

this is embarrassing to type but here goes.

789 clicks. that's ₹2.5 per click, which every marketing blog will tell you is a great rate.

₹0 in orders.

not a single signup. not a single "what is this" message. the entire measurable result of my paid marketing was shruthi messaging me "lol i saw your ad".

here's the math nobody makes you do before you spend. a click is not a person who wants your thing. a click is a person whose thumb was near your ad. 789 thumbs. if even 1% of them had signed up i'd have doubled my seller count. instead the number was zero, and for weeks i thought that meant the ads reached the wrong people.

it didn't. it reached exactly the right people. i know because of shyam.

## the shyam problem

shyam runs a tiffin service in gachibowli. months after my ad campaign died, he signed up. i asked him how he found makeforme.

"i saw your ad. thought it was a scam. then my friend sent me your link and i tried it."

read that again. the ad reached him. the ad was even memorable. and his reaction to a paid ad from an unknown product was the correct reaction every small business owner in india has learned: assume scam.

what converted him was the same ad message arriving through a friend. identical information. different messenger. instant trust.

so my ₹1,978 didn't fail to reach people. it failed to be believable, because nothing a stranger pays to tell you is believable when UPI fraud stories run in the news every week. the ad planted a seed. word of mouth was the only thing that could harvest it. and my analytics dashboard recorded that entire journey as: paid ads, zero conversions.

if you're a small indian founder reading this before your first ad spend: your ads are probably not broken. your category's trust is. budget for the second click, the one a friend sends. that one's free but it takes months.

## the moat i built by accident

once a seller puts their makeforme link on their packaging, they don't leave.

not because the product is amazing. because that link is printed on boxes sitting in customers' homes. changing platforms means reprinting boxes, and reprinting boxes costs more than my ₹99.

the switching cost is physical. i did not plan this. no growth hacker planned this. it happened because sellers treat a printed link as permanent in a way they'd never treat a bio link.

i now think this is the most defensible thing about the product, and it appears nowhere in my feature list. software people underrate how much of india's small commerce runs on printed things. boxes. visiting cards. stickers on tiffin dabbas. every printed link is a tiny contract that says "i'm still here next month".

## the brownie benchmark

one seller in kochi. brownies. three types. classic, walnut, dark chocolate. that's the entire catalogue.

15 to 20 orders a month through her store. she makes more selling brownies than i make from the entire platform. i'm not even mad. i'm studying her.

she posts every day at 6pm. phone pictures on a kitchen counter. no lighting, no flat lays. and people buy.

what she's teaching me: consistency beats production value, and my job is not to give her a prettier store. it's to make sure the store never gets in the way of the 6pm post. every feature i consider now gets the brownie test. does this help her at 6pm, on a phone, tired, between the oven and her feed? if not, it waits.

## what my day looks like

5am wake up. check if any stores broke overnight. they usually did not. fix small things. message sellers. build features they ask for.

no vc money. no co-founder. no office. just me and a macbook air with a cracked screen corner that i keep meaning to fix but never do.

one time i was on a call with a seller and my battery died and she called me back and said "you should charge your phone" and i felt like a child.

## why write this

every indian founder i see posts fake numbers. "crossed 1cr arr in month one!" no you did not. your dad invested and you called it revenue.

i hate that.

so here is the truth, all of it. 13 sellers. ₹1,287 a month. ₹1,978 spent on ads that converted one person, months late, through a friend, for free.

if this fails you will see it here. if it works you will see that too.
